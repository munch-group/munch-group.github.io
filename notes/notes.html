In an early meeting more than 250,000 years ago, the Neanderthal Y chromosome was replaced by the modern human version (Petr et al. n.d.), and the X chromosome introgressed at least as much as the autosomes (Hubisz et al. 2020). In the later meeting around 55,000 years ago, the direction of admixture was reversed. Here the Neanderthal Y chromosome did not introgress into the out-of-Africa anatomically modern humans and the X chromosome introgressed to a much smaller extent (0.3%) than the autosomes (1.4) (Sankararaman et al. 2016). Large regions of the X chromosome today are devoid of Neanderthal introgression - coined as Neanderthal deserts - and overlap with the regions of the X chromosome repeatedly targeted by natural selection in great apes, as discussed above.

Genetic recombination is an essential and mysterious process at the heart of evolution. It shuffles
genetic variants to allow organisms to adapt to new environments and it promotes natural selection
by exposing individual fitness effects of genetic variants. Despite its immense importance, little is
known about the mechanisms that control recombination and how these mechanisms evolve. Taking
our understanding of recombination to a new level will require an approach that is able to measure
the long-term evolution of recombination in individual species and then link this long-term change
in recombination to the responsible genomic changes.

State of the art

In all but a very few cases, we do not know which genes that are responsible for the reduced fertility of hybrids that eventually lead to formation of new species. We do know, however, that when incompatibility does arise, it almost always first appears as reduced fertility of the heterogametic sex (E. g XY mammal males or ZW female birds). This phenomenon has been dubbed Haldane’s rule and represents a century of empirical observations. In the past few years, genomic analyses of individual species groups have confirmed that portions of X and Z chromosomes resist genetic exchange among closely related species. Still, the responsible genes remain unknown, and the evolutionary mechanisms by which incompatibilities arise remain poorly understood. The Dobzhansky-Muller model proposes that incompatibility arises when sets of coevolving inter-dependent genes in each population diverge to a point where combinations of genes from different populations no longer function.
I and others have proposed that a rapid buildup of incompatibilities between sex chromosomes is fueled by an arms race between competing selfish genes on each chromosome (Figure 1A). Only small parts of the sex chromosomes recombine in meiosis. A consequence of this is that genes on sex chromosomes can promote their own transmission to the next generation. E.g. an X-linked gene can control its own selection by any means that leave more viable sperm cells carrying X chromosomes than carrying Y chromosomes. It can do so either by influencing the symmetry of meiosis or by killing sperm cells that carry the Y chromosome (Meiklejohn and Tao 2010). Such meiotic drive will promote the selfish gene variants, even if these are harmful to the organism’s fitness and distort the sex-ratio of offspring. Sex chromosome meiotic drive has been observed in several species (Jaenike 1999), and its genetic basis has recently been proposed in both fruit files (Bachtrog, Ellison et al. 2018) and mice (Rathje, Johnson et al. 2019). In these cases, the responsible genes are ampliconic, and such genes are also found in primates. However, any sex-linked critical gene expressed during or after meiosis has the potential to act as a meiotic driver. I have previously shown that, unlike the remaining genome, the X chromosome was targeted by strong recurrent selection across great ape evolution (Nam, Munch et al. 2015, Munch, Nam et al. 2016). Most recently, I reported that the X chromosomes of non-African humans were hit by bursts of very strong selection between 45 and 55 thousand years ago (Skov, Macia et al. 2018). Together, these observations strongly support the action of meiotic drive.
An arms-race between meiotic drivers on X and Y (or Z and W) will accelerate the coevolution of these genes. This will lead to faster divergence of sex chromosomes between populations and eventually induce reproductive incompatibilities between X and Y chromosomes from different populations (Figure 1). This arms-race has been observed in mice (Rathje, Johnson et al. 2019) and has the potential to explain the widely observed infertility of hybrid XY males and ZW females (Coyne and Orr 2004). It may also explain the observation that genes on the X chromosome contribute disproportionately to sterility (Masly and Presgraves 2007).
I propose that meiotic balance is maintained by antagonizing meiotic drivers on separate sex chromosomes that depress and stabilize each other’s frequency. Perturbations of this equilibrium will trigger strong temporary selection on individual meiotic drivers. In ongoing work, I use forward simulations to show that competing meiotic drivers can be kept at low frequency by frequency-dependent selection and that the resulting dynamic equilibrium maintains meiotic balance (Figure 2). I also find that admixture or demographic perturbations disturb this equilibrium, triggering strong temporary selection on individual driver variants. The hypothesis can thus explain my observations of strong selection on the X chromosome coinciding with the exodus of modern humans from Africa (Skov, Macià et al. 2018). The hypothesis also reconciles strong selection on individual meiotic drivers with empirical observations of stable and even sex ratios in most species.
Because meiotic drive reduces fertility and distorts the sex-ratio, I propose that these detrimental effects trigger compensating adaptive selection on normal genes across the genome to restore fertility and an even sex ratio. Such compensation may explain why genes in tumor suppression pathways are among the most rapidly adapting genes in the genome (Nielsen, Bustamante et al. 2005). This adaptation is unexpected because these genes perform cellular tasks that serve core cellular functions, which are not expected to require much if any, adaptive evolution. Such genes are found in the TP53 signaling pathway that controls DNA damage response and apoptosis. This regulatory network strongly overlaps networks of genes controlling recombination and meiosis. Many of these genes are located on the X chromosome, and my recent report (Skov, Macià et al. 2018) suggests that several of them may be meiotic drivers (E.g., HUWE1, and SMC1A). I hypothesize that the selection of such X-linked drivers may perturb the overlapping networks to induce apoptosis of Y carrying sperm, thus producing a preferential transmission of X carrying sperm. This detrimental effect on male fertility is then expected to trigger compensating adaptive evolution of other genes in the same networks to counter this regulatory perturbation and restore male fertility and an even sex ratio of children. In a pilot study addressing this hypothesis, I found that the 3p21.3 genomic region has been affected by recurrent adaptation, and it indeed harbors an abundance of genes in the TP53 network: IP6K2, USP4, HYAL2, HYAL3, and PRKAR2A.
I thus present a general model of the genetic basis of speciation and its derived effects across the genome. The model not only explains existing empirical evidence but also has the potential to guide new insights into speciation and fertility.

Objectives and scientific impact

Although the genetic basis of hybrid incompatibility will vary across species groups, each one is an individual outcome of a similar evolutionary process. By accumulating evidence across species groups, I will thus be able to learn which classes of genes and regulatory networks that are responsible, and to identify general mechanisms by which mutation, selection, and recombination contribute to the accumulation of incompatibility. I will also be able to detect differences across groups of species, attributed to the sex-determination system (XY vs. ZW), population size, dispersal range, and sperm competition. In each species, I will identify candidate meiotic drivers by locating strong selection on X and Z chromosomes. I will link each candidate driver gene to hybrid incompatibility by testing if their exchange between species is associated with reduced fitness.
This project is the first to link natural selection in the form of meiotic drive to hybrid incompatibility across many groups of species. By answering fundamental evolutionary questions, the results from this project will impact many connected research fields. Being able to evaluate from genomic data alone, which populations are emerging new species, will inform biodiversity management. Understanding how tumor-suppression adapt to counter regulatory disruptions by meiotic drivers will provide new insights into the regulatory dependencies of tumor-suppressors. New insights into the sensitivities of male fertility will identify new genes and pathways as potential targets for explaining and treating the subfertility of one-third of Danish men.

Data

To maximize inference power, I will analyze all available full genome data sets from species groups with different characteristics, such as XY or ZW karyotype (E. g. birds or bats), dispersal range (E. g. birds vs monkeys), population size (E. g. mosquitos vs polar bears), and divergence time (E. g. ape species vs ape populations). Analysis workflows will be designed to allow the seamless addition of the many additional genomic data sets that will become available during the project period. I now have access to the following full genome data, either through public access, or early access through collaborations with (Guojie Zhang DK, Jeffrey Rodgers, US, and Tomas Marques-Bonet, Spain): >1000 humans across 51 populations, 43 Gorillas across three subspecies, 26 orangutans across two species, 58 chimpanzees across four subspecies, 13 bonobos, 105 baboons across five subspecies, 163 vervet monkeys across five populations, 89 bears across two species, 60 bats across three species, 56 finches across four species, 60 penguins across three species, 690 swordtail fish across four species, 1142 mosquitos across two species and six populations. With a few exceptions, each data set includes both pure unadmixed and hybrid populations/species. In addition, I have access to a single genome of a large number of species: 55 primates, 344 birds, 854 butterflies, and 81 flatworms and roundworms. Where genome samples from admixture zones are not available, I will acquire such samples through collaborations and perform supplementary genome sequencing myself. The project budget covers sequencing of 300 such samples.

Automation of reproducible HPC workflows

Goal: establish automated HPC workflows. The key to the success of this project is the scale of parallel analyzes so that each of the computationally demanding analyses described below can be applied to any number of large data sets on our HPC facility (GenomeDK). This requires a high level of automation that I will achieve by embedding all analyses in one pipeline. To this end, I will use a workflow library (GWF) that we have developed at the Bioinformatics Research Centre (BiRC). This tool graphs dependencies between analyses to maintain the integrity of the pipeline as analyses update and data sets are added. All steps from the initial curation of raw data to the final summary analyses as Jupyter notebooks will be embedded in this workflow. I will use Conda (by Anaconda) to maintain reproducible library dependencies, and all developed analyses will be distributed as self-contained Conda packages or Docker images. This allows collaborating research groups, with access to proprietary or sensitive data, to run the analyses in-house and only contribute anonymized meta-data to this project.

Meiotic drivers in living species

Goal: develop tailored methods for inference of sweeps. Recent adaptive evolution manifests as selective sweeps of genetic variation. However, meiotic drive may not be recent and is expected to produce incomplete sweeps, which leave a similar, but more subtle, genetic signature. I will develop new specialized methods able to identify this particular signal.
I will develop a suite of population genetic summary statistics, each tailored to the particular inference of candidate regions subject to non-recent incomplete sweeps. This will be done in collaboration with David Enard (University of Arizona), who has already developed one such statistic, hapDAF, which quantifies linkage of high-frequency variants. This statistic has very good power to detect simulated non-recent partial sweeps. Tailored summary statistics will initially be combined into a composite score using a framework following (Grossman, Shlyakhter et al. 2010). I will then develop a more powerful approach by training deep convolutional neural networks on vectors of summary statistics to classify each genomic region as subjected to a sweep or not.
For a detailed analysis of causal variants in swept regions, I will develop a tailored method, which extends a recently published model (Stern, Wilton et al. 2019). This method integrates over marginal genealogies at a focal site to test if inferred frequency trajectories of candidate alleles are unlikely without selection. I will modify the likelihood model to describe a confined non-recent episode of strong selection. By incorporating demography in the null model, this approach is also able to distinguish the similar effects of population bottlenecks and selective sweeps. In addition to allowing a more powerful estimation of selection strength, this method will allow me to test my hypothesis that strong selection on meiotic drivers is episodic and does not lead to the fixation of drivers. This test is possible because the developed approach will enable me to estimate the historical allele frequency of meiotic drivers.
I will apply both methods to all population genomic diversity data for all available species and populations to identify candidate meiotic drivers on the X and Z sex chromosomes. To address the hypothesis that demographic events may trigger strong temporary selection on drivers, I will estimate the historical population size of each species, using HMM-based methods (SMC++, and MSMC). I will then relate the strength and number of identified sweeps to the occurrence of a demographic event such as population bottlenecks.

Meiotic drivers in ancestral species

Goal: find sweeps in ancestral species. Whereas patterns of genetic diversity reveal the recent action of selection, I need to analyze genomic patterns of divergence between multiple species to reveal the effect of the selection across long evolutionary time scales. The common ancestor of two sequences sampled from different species is much older than the time they stopped interbreeding. If the population size of the ancestral species is large, the common sequence ancestor may reach all the way back into an additional species shared by a third species. In this case, the topology of individual gene trees may differ from the topology of the species tree. This is a phenomenon called incomplete lineage sorting (ILS), which I have worked on for many years (Figure 3).
For all relevant trios of closely related species, I will use a coalescent hidden Markov model (Dutheil, et al. 2009), to infer the gene tree topology for each consecutive segment of the aligned genomes using both Viterbi and posterior decoding of each trained HMM. The vast amount of data produced will be stored efficiently as tree-sequence data structures using tsKit. Strong selective sweeps, produced by meiotic drivers in ancestral species, will induce immediate ancestry of lineages. Completed hard selective sweeps are thus readily detectable as wide genomic regions without any ILS (Figure 3C). Soft or partial sweeps are visible as wide genomic regions with a homogeneously reduced, but non-zero, proportion of ILS. I can thus test my hypothesis that meiotic drivers sweep when the equilibrium of antagonizing meiotic drivers is disturbed. In this case, soft sweeps (selection on pre-existing variants) and non-recent partial (aborted) sweeps should be much more prevalent on the X and Z chromosomes than on the remaining genome. I will evaluate the significance of each low-ILS region by comparing its size, distribution of segment lengths, and autocorrelation of ILS proportion, to empirical background distributions obtained from the entire genome (accounting for the difference in effective population sizes of sex chromosomes and autosomes).
I will identify sweeps on all chromosomes of each entire ancestral genome. However, in line with my previous work (Dutheil, Munch et al. 2015, Nam, Munch et al. 2015, Munch, Nam et al. 2016), I expect to find most of them on the X and Z chromosomes. Indeed, in a preliminary unpublished analysis of ILS on the X chromosome of the human-chimpanzee and human-orangutan ancestors I find several strong sweeps in these ancestors. I will detect individual sweeps in all ancestral species, but also combine gene tree information for all species trios, to produce gene trees spanning the entire 55 primates, 344 birds, 854 butterflies, and 81 worms. This way I will combine evidence of selection across ancestral species.

The time scale of reproductive isolation

Goal: learn when admixture occurred between diverging populations. I will develop and apply a new method to infer historical admixture between species and populations. Knowledge of such admixture is required to test if admixture ceases for sex-chromosomes before it does for the remaining genome. It is also necessary to test the hypothesis that events of secondary contact between diverging population trigger strong meiotic drive, as suggested by my recent report on the human expansion from Africa (Skov, Macià et al. 2018).
Recent admixture between species or populations will be inferred using published software (see below) and dated from the length distribution of genomic admixture segments. State of the art for estimating older admixture exploits the covariance structure of genetic diversity between populations. These methods, however, perform a greedy search for an admixture graph with a good fit to data and do not provide an estimate of confidence in the obtained result. I propose a new approach to inference that does not have these shortcomings. Recent work has shown that the coalescent, which models the ancestry of sampled genes, can elegantly be described using phase-type distributions (Hobolth, Siri-Jegousse et al. 2019). In this new formulation, it is possible to obtain the full (multivariate) distribution of the frequency spectrum of variants (site frequency spectrum) in a single population. Together with a MSc student, I have shown that the phase-type formulation maps directly to directed acyclic graphs for the relevant models. This insight dramatically reduces the running time, allowing for up to 50 samples and thus powerful inference. Using this approach, I have also shown a general expression for the joint distribution of branch lengths of gene tree lineages with different numbers of descendants. Preliminary results show that I will be able to compute the joint likelihood of the site frequency spectra from two populations with common ancestry. I will extend this simple setting to three populations with the possibility of admixture between species. Because we can compute the likelihood of an observed multi-population SFS given a species-admixture graph, we can use Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) to obtain posterior probabilities of such graphs as well as the marginal probabilities of individual admixture events. Having obtained an admixture graph, I will estimate the dates of species splits and admixture events using a coalescent HMM.

The genomic location of reproductive barriers

Goal: learn if candidate meiotic drivers are the first genes that induce hybrid incompatibility.
Having identified candidate meiotic drivers, I will investigate if their rapid evolution induces a lower fitness of hybrids. In this case, the candidate genes will resist admixture and produce genomic regions around them that are free from genomic segments introduced from other populations or subspecies – so-called “admixture deserts”. There is ample empirical evidence that both X and Z chromosomes harbor an abundance of such wide regions. However, the regions contain a large number of potentially responsible genes. Because the size of the admixture desert on each side of a causal gene is the outcome of a stochastic process, I will be able to sharply reduce the number of gene candidates by limiting the search to admixture deserts shared by many related species. Prediction of admixed segments along the genome will be made using published methods. To detect recent admixture between sampled populations, I will use the HMM-based ChromoPainter and HMM+MCMC based RELATE and ARGweaver-D to identify admixed genomic segments. To detect admixture segments contributed from unsampled populations, I will use a method using conditional random fields. I will also use the HMM-based method, which I used to detect archaic introgression into modern humans in my recent report of strong selection on the human X chromosome.

Invasion of meiotic drivers

Goal: identify meiotic drivers that invaded other populations. Perhaps counterintuitively, the meiotic drivers, which fuel accelerated coevolution of sex-linked genes and the buildup of incompatibility between populations, can themselves invade other subspecies (Meiklejohn, Landeen et al. 2018). This will happen if they are able to act as meiotic drivers in the recipient subspecies. Such introgressions constitute a separate type of observations, which can be used to identify meiotic drivers. To infer such introgressions, I will develop a new approach based on a newly published study (VolcanoFinder), which models a characteristic but weak signature in genetic diversity. In the particular case of non-African humans, the statistical power can be increased ten-fold by analyzing only genetic variation unique to non-Africans. In the many other species, the same trick can be used for a similar performance boost. I will also develop an approach based on called genomic admixture segments that quantify if the sharing of segments is more prevalent than expected from population structure. These methods will be applied to all available admixing species. Historical invasions between ancestral species will be identified from ILS patterns as extended regions showing a gene tree different from the species tree. Here, I will call individual ancestral invasions, but also to identify the accumulated signal of repeated invasions of a gene across a species tree from a significantly elevated amount of ILS.

Compensating autosomal adaptation

Goal: learn how sex-linked drive affects adaptation across the genome. Many of the most rapidly adapting genes in the genome are part of sizeable regulatory gene networks linked to tumor suppression. I will expand the inference of selective sweeps in each species to include the entire genome. This will allow me to investigate if the adapting genes are in the same pathways as meiotic drivers and thus build support for my hypothesis that meiotic drive triggers compensating adaptation of these autosomal genes. This will be done using published software for signaling pathway impact analysis (SPIA).
In a complementary approach, I will test if the same is true for adaptive evolution over long evolutionary time scales. I will compare genetic polymorphism and divergence at functional and nonfunctional sites to estimate the rate of adaptive evolution of each gene in the genome. To this end, I will use a method, which I recently applied to estimate the amount of adaptive evolution driven by virus interaction (Castellano, Uricchio et al. 2019). I will then subject the network of known protein interactions from the KEGG, REACTOME, and SPRING gene network databases to a network propagation algorithm. This approach will allow me to test if recurrently adapting autosomal genes are more strongly connected to candidate meiotic drivers than expected (when properly accounting for network architecture and tissue expression).

Meta-analyses

Goal: accumulate and contrast evidence across species to produce new insight. In contrast to the complex computational analyzes of each species, the results produced are simple. The inference of selective sweeps will produce genomic regions containing a small number of candidate genes. Inference of hybrid incompatibility will produce genomic regions where admixture between populations is precluded by reduced fitness (admixture deserts). Although each genomic region is associated with a measure of significance and analysis meta-information, the size of meta datasets will be small enough to handle in Jupyter notebooks hosted from a powerful HPC compute node. This allows for manual inspection, visualization, and data analysis of the continually accumulating results.
I will use a published statistical method for cross-species gene set analysis (XGSA) to identify pathways and regulatory networks with an overrepresentation of candidate genes. By testing all candidate genes for enrichment of ontologies terms, I will further establish which cellular functions are primarily responsible for hybrid incompatibility. I will combine these analyses with inferred species divergence, admixture, and demography, as well as the ecological, physical, and behavioral properties of each species. That way, I will learn if the responsible genes, pathways, or characteristic cellular functions differ in species with XY and ZW sex-determination system, or if they differ at early and late stages of speciation. I will also learn if meiotic drive is more frequent, and speciation rate faster, in species with no sperm competition. This is expected because detrimental effects on fertility have a smaller effect on fitness in such species.

Project feasibility

The progress and completion of this project rely on access to an HPC facility, which is available through the Bioinformatics Research Centre (GenomeDK). All data is either publicly available or available through collaborators. The feasibility of the proposed methods has been ensured through pilot projects of my MSc and project students. It is possible that one or more of my hypotheses are not proven true. However, the bulk of empirical evidence leaves it unquestionable that sex-chromosomes hold the key to understanding speciation. Hence even in the unlikely case that my hypotheses are wrong, the scale and breadth of this project will reveal the true mechanisms. In either case, the general interest in these questions will ensure publications of high impact.

Work and publication plan

The research project constitutes the work of three postdocs and two Ph.D. students. One two-year postdoc will work on new methods for the inference of selective sweeps from diversity data. One two-year postdoc will work on autosomal adaptation in response to meiotic drive. One three-year postdoc will work on hybrid incompatibility and invasion of meiotic drivers. One Ph.D. student will work on the inference of selection and admixture using incomplete lineage sorting. One Ph.D. student will work on phase-type distributions and admixture graphs. All students and postdocs will take part in workflow integration and meta-analyses. See Figure 4 for an activity plan. Students and postdocs will lead publications of their own methods or analysis work and coauthor papers on results from integrating all analyses into the broader workflow.

Ph.D. students will be enrolled in the Graduate School of Science (GSST) and Technology at Aarhus University. Postdocs and Ph.D. students will be part of a study environment with international postdocs and Ph.D. students from other groups, all working on evolutionary questions using similar theory and methodology. In addition to the mandatory activities associated with GSST, Ph.D. students at our research center are fully integrated into all academic activities such as seminars and journal clubs. Postdocs will be encouraged to participate in my courses in population genomics and data science. Project students and MSc students will be trained in the context of this project and be assigned small self-contained subprojects addressing scientific questions embedded in the larger research strategy.
